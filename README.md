# Mathematical Methods for Physics

This repository contains my self written lecture notes on the mathematical methods used in classical mechanics, electrodynamics, and theoretical physics. The notes were developed as a structured companion for first year physics students and are intended to connect formal mathematics with the physical ideas that motivate it.

The current manuscript contains approximately 475 pages and covers calculus, vector analysis, complex analysis, differential equations, partial differential equations, linear algebra, tensor analysis, the calculus of variations, field theory, electromagnetism, differential forms, Hamiltonian mechanics, numerical methods, and special relativity.

## Purpose

Many physics courses introduce mathematical tools only when they are immediately needed. This can make the subject feel fragmented. These notes instead organize the mathematics into a continuous framework, while keeping physical interpretation and applications visible throughout.

The main goals are:

1. To provide the mathematical background needed for classical mechanics and electrodynamics.
2. To explain derivations rather than present formulas without context.
3. To connect calculus, geometry, differential equations, variational methods, and field theory.
4. To serve as a long term reference for later courses in theoretical physics.

## Selected Physical Applications

The notes include applications to:

1. Simple harmonic motion and driven oscillations
2. Pendulum motion and elliptic integrals
3. RLC circuits
4. Heat conduction and diffusion
5. Vibrating strings and wave propagation
6. Electrostatics and Green's functions
7. Coupled spring systems
8. Quantum mechanical eigenvalue problems
9. Curvilinear coordinate systems
10. Relativistic mechanics
11. Classical field theory
12. Maxwell theory from an action principle

## Intended Audience

These notes are written primarily for undergraduate physics students who are studying classical mechanics, electrodynamics, mathematical methods, or introductory theoretical physics.

Some sections are more advanced than a standard first year course. They may be read selectively and used as references when the corresponding topics appear in later courses.
